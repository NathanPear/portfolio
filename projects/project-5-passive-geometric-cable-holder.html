PROJECT 5 / MECHANICAL

Passive Geometric Cable Holder

A static rectangular spiral cable holder securing wires through natural geometry and rigidity without material fatigue.

  • Role: mechanical concept and geometric design
  • Focus: zero-wear kinematic constraint
  • Constraint: no moving parts or fatigue-prone elements

Project Details

Problem and Context

Traditional cable holders rely on moving parts or compliant mechanisms that suffer from material fatigue and eventual failure. Mechanical clips often cause abrasive wear on cable jackets or plastically deform over time, rendering them useless.

To address these issues, the design challenge was to create a permanent, zero-maintenance retention system that operates purely through static physical structures and kinematic constraints.

Scope and Constraints
  • Zero-wear architecture with no moving parts
  • Prevent unintentional cable exit using wire stiffness
  • Allow free sliding along the cable diameter
  • Accommodate standard cable and connector head sizes

Approach

What I Built
  • A static rectangular spiral design that secures the cable using only its natural geometry
  • Zero-wear architecture removing all dynamic components to ensure a permanent solution
  • Sized internal channel acting as a hard stop for the connector head while allowing wire sliding
  • A 360° spiral path that effectively restricts five out of six Degrees of Freedom
Cross-Disciplinary Read

This design shifts the problem of cable retention from material compliance to spatial kinematics. By utilizing the natural rigidity of the cable to prevent self-unthreading, it achieves permanent constraint without relying on elastic deformation.

This project is legible to reviewers interested in static mechanism design, kinematic constraints, and maintenance-free consumer products.

Outcome

Result

The resulting cable holder provides a zero-wear solution that secures cables indefinitely without degradation, material fatigue, or loss of holding force.

By eliminating dynamic components, the spiral design ensures a lifetime of reliable use with zero failure points.

Takeaways
  • Static geometry can solve problems usually reserved for moving mechanisms
  • Restricting degrees of freedom is a powerful design pattern
  • Simplifying designs by removing components increases reliability
