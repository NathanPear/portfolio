PROJECT 2 / MECHANICAL

Retractable Cane Stabilizer

A 3D-printed attachment that keeps a cane upright when parked, without changing the way it feels during normal walking.

  • Role: mechanical concept, CAD, and packaging
  • Focus: stability, ergonomics, and easy deployment
  • Constraint: preserve the cane's everyday feel

Project Details

Problem and Context

Standard canes tend to fall over when they are set down. That makes them awkward to park and more annoying to use in everyday environments.

The design challenge was to add stability only when the cane is at rest, while leaving the walking experience unchanged.

Scope and Constraints
  • Provide a stable parked position
  • Fold out of the way during walking
  • Stay usable for limited grip strength or arthritis
  • Fit within a 3D-printed mechanical assembly

Approach

What I Built
  • A ratchet-and-pawl deployment mechanism for incremental movement
  • A large tactile reset lever to make the interaction easier to use
  • Parametric CAD models that support fit and tolerance changes
  • Friction-fit details sized for 3D-printing reality
Cross-Disciplinary Read

This is a product-design story as much as it is a mechanical one. The interesting part is the user experience: the device has to feel invisible while walking and immediately useful when parked.

That makes the project legible to reviewers from mechanical design, industrial design, accessibility, and prototyping backgrounds.

Outcome

Result

The design provides a parkable cane concept that supports itself when set down and returns to a compact form for normal use.

That preserves ergonomics while solving the everyday annoyance of a cane that refuses to stay upright.

Takeaways
  • Accessibility problems often become mechanism problems
  • Good CAD starts with user constraints, not geometry alone
  • Small interactions matter when grip strength is limited
